# Methods Guide

## Overview {#overview}

Choosing and applying an appropriate method is one of the most challenging parts of writing a thesis. This guide covers the methods most commonly used by students I supervise, with practical guidance on how to apply each one in your thesis.

Each section below explains what the method is, when to use it, how to apply it step by step, and what to read. The methods are not mutually exclusive — many theses combine two or more (for example, comparative case study design with process tracing, or framing analysis with corpus building).

For general guidance on selecting a method, see the [Getting Started Guide — Step 4: Building Your Analytical Framework]({{ '/getting-started/#step-4' | relative_url }}). For how your method choice is assessed, see [Assessment Standards — Application of Knowledge]({{ '/assessment-standards/#application-of-knowledge' | relative_url }}).

## The Methods {#the-methods}

<details id="comparative-case-study" markdown="0">
<summary>Comparative Case Study Design</summary>
<div class="details-content">

<p class="step-intro">Comparative case study design is a structured approach to analyzing two or more cases in order to identify causal relationships, test theories, or build new explanations. It is the most widely used method across all four programs I supervise.</p>

<div class="step-objectives">
<p>Objectives</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand the logic of comparative case study design and its foundations</li>
<li>Distinguish between Most Similar Systems Design (MSSD) and Most Different Systems Design (MDSD)</li>
<li>Learn how to select cases systematically</li>
<li>Apply comparative design to your own thesis</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>What is comparative case study design?</h4>

<p>At its core, comparative case study design asks: <em>what can we learn by comparing cases that are similar in some ways but differ in others?</em> The method traces back to John Stuart Mill's (1843) <strong>Method of Agreement</strong> and <strong>Method of Difference</strong> — two logical strategies for identifying the causes of outcomes by comparing cases that share or differ on key variables.</p>

<p>In comparative politics and international relations, these became formalized as <strong>Most Similar Systems Design (MSSD)</strong> and <strong>Most Different Systems Design (MDSD)</strong> by Przeworski and Teune (1970). Lijphart (1971) further developed the comparative method as a distinct research strategy alongside experimental, statistical, and case study approaches.</p>

<h4>Key concepts</h4>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Concept</th><th>What it means</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Most Similar Systems Design (MSSD)</strong></td><td>Select cases that are as similar as possible on background variables but differ on the outcome you want to explain. The logic: if two similar cases produce different outcomes, the cause must lie in the variables where they differ.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Most Different Systems Design (MDSD)</strong></td><td>Select cases that differ on most background variables but share the same outcome. The logic: if very different cases produce the same outcome, the cause must lie in the variables they share.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Structured, focused comparison</strong></td><td>A method for comparing cases using the same set of questions across each case, enabling systematic comparison (George &amp; Bennett 2005).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Case selection</strong></td><td>The deliberate, justified choice of which cases to study. Seawright and Gerring (2008) identify seven techniques: typical, diverse, extreme, deviant, influential, most similar, and most different.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>When to use it</h4>

<ul>
<li>Your research question asks <em>why</em> something happens in one place but not another, or why similar contexts produce different outcomes</li>
<li>You have two or more cases that can be meaningfully compared</li>
<li>You want to test a theory across cases or build a new explanation from cross-case patterns</li>
<li>Your data allows you to identify key similarities and differences between cases</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>BA vs MA:</strong> BA theses typically compare two cases with a clear justification for the comparison. MA theses are expected to engage more deeply with the logic of case selection, control variables, and scope conditions.</p>

<h4>How to apply it in your thesis</h4>

<ol>
<li><strong>Define your research question</strong> — What outcome or phenomenon are you trying to explain?</li>
<li><strong>Identify your universe of cases</strong> — What is the broader set of cases your question applies to?</li>
<li><strong>Select cases with justification</strong> — Use MSSD, MDSD, or another case selection technique (Seawright &amp; Gerring 2008). Explain <em>why</em> these cases and not others.</li>
<li><strong>Identify key variables</strong> — What are the independent variables, dependent variables, and control variables?</li>
<li><strong>Structure your comparison</strong> — Apply the same analytical questions to each case. Present findings in a way that makes the comparison explicit.</li>
<li><strong>Draw cross-case conclusions</strong> — What does the comparison reveal? Do the cases confirm, disconfirm, or complicate your expectations?</li>
</ol>

<h4>Example from the literature</h4>

<p>Hur (2022) compares South Korea and Taiwan — two East Asian democracies with similar political trajectories (authoritarian pasts, democratic transitions, contested national identities) — to examine how national narratives shape civic duty differently. This is an MSSD design: the cases are maximally similar on background variables, allowing Hur to isolate the effect of divergent national narratives on democratic participation.</p>

<p>Similarly, Denney, Steinhardt, and Bhowmick (2026) compare Taiwan and South Korea as most-similar cases to investigate identity conformity pressures in divided societies, showing how social desirability bias leads citizens in both countries to overstate national pride — but through different mechanisms.</p>

<div class="tip-box">
<p><strong>Don't just compare two cases because they interest you.</strong> Your case selection must be <em>justified</em> — explain why these cases constitute a meaningful comparison, what the logic of selection is (MSSD, MDSD, or other), and what you can and cannot conclude from the comparison.</p>
</div>

<div class="reflection-box">
<p>Ask yourself</p>
<ul>
<li>What is your universe of cases, and why did you select the ones you did?</li>
<li>Are you using MSSD, MDSD, or a different selection logic? Can you explain it?</li>
<li>What variables are you holding constant, and what varies across your cases?</li>
<li>Are there alternative cases that might challenge your conclusions?</li>
<li>Is your comparison structured — are you asking the same questions of each case?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="exercise-box">
<p>Exercise</p>
<p>Write a case selection memo (300–500 words) that includes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Your research question</li>
<li>The universe of cases your question applies to</li>
<li>Which cases you have selected and the logic of selection (MSSD, MDSD, or other)</li>
<li>The key variables: what varies, what is held constant</li>
<li>Any limitations of your case selection</li>
</ol>
<p>Bring this to your next supervision meeting.</p>
</div>

<h4>Key readings</h4>

<ul>
<li><strong>Seawright, J., &amp; Gerring, J. (2008).</strong> "Case selection techniques in case study research." <em>Political Research Quarterly</em>, 61(2), 294–308. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1177/1065912907313077">DOI</a> — The essential guide to systematic case selection.</li>
<li><strong>Przeworski, A., &amp; Teune, H. (1970).</strong> <em>The Logic of Comparative Social Inquiry.</em> Wiley-Interscience. — Where MSSD and MDSD originate.</li>
<li><strong>George, A. L., &amp; Bennett, A. (2005).</strong> <em>Case Studies and Theory Development in the Social Sciences.</em> MIT Press. — The standard reference for structured, focused comparison and within-case analysis.</li>
<li><strong>Lijphart, A. (1971).</strong> "Comparative politics and the comparative method." <em>American Political Science Review</em>, 65(3), 682–693. <a href="https://doi.org/10.2307/1955513">DOI</a> — Foundational article on the comparative method as a research strategy.</li>
<li><strong>Hur, A. (2022).</strong> <em>Narratives of Civic Duty: How National Stories Shape Democracy in Asia.</em> Cornell University Press. — An exemplary MSSD comparison of South Korea and Taiwan.</li>
</ul>

</div>
</details>

<details id="process-tracing" markdown="0">
<summary>Process Tracing</summary>
<div class="details-content">

<p class="step-intro">Process tracing is a within-case method for identifying causal mechanisms — the steps and sequences through which a cause produces an effect. It complements comparative design (which works across cases) by looking <em>inside</em> a single case.</p>

<div class="step-objectives">
<p>Objectives</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand what process tracing is and what it can (and cannot) do</li>
<li>Distinguish between theory-testing, theory-building, and explaining-outcome process tracing</li>
<li>Learn the types of evidence used in process tracing</li>
<li>Apply process tracing in your thesis</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>What is process tracing?</h4>

<p>Process tracing examines the chain of events, decisions, and mechanisms that connect a cause to an outcome within a single case. Rather than asking <em>whether</em> X caused Y (as in a statistical study), process tracing asks <em>how</em> X caused Y — by tracing the causal process step by step.</p>

<p>Think of it like detective work: you have a suspect (a proposed cause) and an outcome (the crime). Process tracing gathers evidence about the intermediate steps to determine whether the suspect actually did it, and how.</p>

<h4>Key concepts</h4>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Concept</th><th>What it means</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Causal mechanism</strong></td><td>The process or pathway through which a cause produces an effect. Not just a correlation, but the <em>how</em> and <em>why</em>.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Theory-testing</strong></td><td>Start with a theory about the causal mechanism, then look for evidence that confirms or disconfirms each step (Beach &amp; Pedersen 2019).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Theory-building</strong></td><td>Start with an unexplained outcome and work backwards to identify the mechanism (Beach &amp; Pedersen 2019).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Explaining-outcome</strong></td><td>Build a case-specific explanation of a particular outcome, combining multiple mechanisms if needed (Beach &amp; Pedersen 2019).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Causal process observations (CPOs)</strong></td><td>Pieces of evidence about the causal process itself — not just inputs and outputs, but what happened in between (Collier 2011).</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>Types of process tracing tests</h4>

<p>Mahoney (2012) formalized four types of tests used to evaluate evidence in process tracing:</p>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Test</th><th>Logic</th><th>Example</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Straw-in-the-wind</strong></td><td>Neither necessary nor sufficient — weakly supports or weakens a hypothesis</td><td>A politician attended a meeting with lobbyists (consistent with influence, but not conclusive)</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Hoop test</strong></td><td>Necessary but not sufficient — the hypothesis must pass this test to survive, but passing doesn't confirm it</td><td>If a policy changed, there must be a record of a vote (no record = hypothesis fails)</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Smoking gun</strong></td><td>Sufficient but not necessary — if found, strongly confirms the hypothesis</td><td>A leaked memo showing a direct order to change the policy</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Doubly decisive</strong></td><td>Both necessary and sufficient — conclusively confirms or eliminates</td><td>Rare in social science; analogous to DNA evidence at a crime scene</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>When to use it</h4>

<ul>
<li>Your research question asks <em>how</em> or <em>why</em> something happened in a specific case</li>
<li>You want to identify the causal mechanism linking a cause to an outcome</li>
<li>You have rich, detailed evidence about the sequence of events within a case</li>
<li>You are using it alongside comparative design — comparing <em>across</em> cases and tracing <em>within</em> cases</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>BA vs MA:</strong> BA theses can use process tracing in a simplified form — tracing a sequence of events to support a causal argument. MA theses should engage with the formal logic (theory-testing vs. theory-building), types of evidence, and the distinction between correlation and mechanism.</p>

<h4>How to apply it in your thesis</h4>

<ol>
<li><strong>Specify your causal claim</strong> — What cause and outcome are you connecting? What mechanism do you propose?</li>
<li><strong>Map the expected causal chain</strong> — Break the mechanism into discrete, observable steps.</li>
<li><strong>Identify the evidence needed</strong> — For each step, what evidence would you expect to find if the mechanism operates as proposed?</li>
<li><strong>Gather evidence systematically</strong> — Use documents, interviews, archival records, media sources, or other data to look for evidence at each step.</li>
<li><strong>Evaluate the evidence</strong> — Apply the process tracing tests. Does the evidence pass hoop tests? Are there smoking guns? What alternative explanations remain?</li>
<li><strong>Present the traced process</strong> — Write up the causal chain with evidence at each step. Be transparent about where evidence is strong and where it is thin.</li>
</ol>

<div class="tip-box">
<p><strong>Process tracing is not narrative.</strong> Simply telling the story of what happened is not process tracing. You must specify the <em>mechanism</em> you are testing, identify what <em>evidence</em> each step requires, and evaluate whether that evidence <em>confirms or disconfirms</em> the proposed mechanism.</p>
</div>

<div class="reflection-box">
<p>Ask yourself</p>
<ul>
<li>What causal mechanism are you proposing? Can you break it into discrete steps?</li>
<li>What evidence would you expect to find at each step if the mechanism operates as proposed?</li>
<li>What alternative mechanisms could explain the same outcome?</li>
<li>Are you testing an existing theory, building a new one, or explaining a specific outcome?</li>
<li>Do you have access to sufficiently detailed evidence to trace the process?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="exercise-box">
<p>Exercise</p>
<p>Write a process tracing plan (300–500 words) that includes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Your proposed causal mechanism (what cause → what outcome, through what steps)</li>
<li>A diagram or numbered list of the expected causal chain</li>
<li>For each step, the type of evidence you will look for and where you will find it</li>
<li>At least one alternative mechanism you will test against</li>
</ol>
<p>Bring this to your next supervision meeting.</p>
</div>

<h4>Key readings</h4>

<ul>
<li><strong>Beach, D., &amp; Pedersen, R. B. (2019).</strong> <em>Process-Tracing Methods: Foundations and Guidelines</em> (2nd ed.). University of Michigan Press. <a href="https://doi.org/10.3998/mpub.10072208">DOI</a> — The most comprehensive guide, covering all three variants.</li>
<li><strong>Collier, D. (2011).</strong> "Understanding process tracing." <em>PS: Political Science &amp; Politics</em>, 44(4), 823–830. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1017/S1049096511001429">DOI</a> — Accessible introduction. <a href="https://polisci.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/people/u3827/Understanding%20Process%20Tracing.pdf">Free PDF</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Bennett, A., &amp; Checkel, J. T. (Eds.). (2015).</strong> <em>Process Tracing: From Metaphor to Analytic Tool.</em> Cambridge University Press. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781139858472">DOI</a> — Comprehensive edited volume on formalizing process tracing.</li>
<li><strong>Mahoney, J. (2012).</strong> "The logic of process tracing tests in the social sciences." <em>Sociological Methods &amp; Research</em>, 41(4), 570–597. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1177/0049124112437709">DOI</a> — Formalizes the four types of process tracing tests.</li>
<li><strong>George, A. L., &amp; Bennett, A. (2005).</strong> <em>Case Studies and Theory Development in the Social Sciences.</em> MIT Press. — Chapter 10 covers process tracing as within-case analysis.</li>
</ul>

</div>
</details>

<details id="framing-analysis" markdown="0">
<summary>Framing Analysis</summary>
<div class="details-content">

<p class="step-intro">Framing analysis examines how issues are presented — what is emphasized, what is left out, and how this shapes understanding. It is especially common in theses that analyze media coverage, political communication, or policy debates.</p>

<div class="step-objectives">
<p>Objectives</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand what frames are and how they work</li>
<li>Distinguish between inductive and deductive approaches to frame identification</li>
<li>Learn how to code texts for frames systematically</li>
<li>Apply framing analysis to your own thesis</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>What is framing?</h4>

<p>Entman (1993) defined framing as selecting <em>"some aspects of a perceived reality and making them more salient in a communicating text, in such a way as to promote a particular problem definition, causal interpretation, moral evaluation, and/or treatment recommendation."</em> These four elements — problem definition, causal diagnosis, moral judgment, and treatment recommendation — remain the standard analytical dimensions of a frame.</p>

<p>Framing matters because the same issue can be presented in fundamentally different ways. A protest can be framed as a democratic exercise or as a public order threat. An economic policy can be framed through a growth lens or an inequality lens. Framing analysis reveals these choices and their implications.</p>

<h4>Key concepts</h4>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Concept</th><th>What it means</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Frame</strong></td><td>A central organizing idea that provides meaning to an issue by selecting and emphasizing certain aspects over others (Gamson &amp; Modigliani 1989).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Generic frames</strong></td><td>Frames that appear across issues and over time. Semetko and Valkenburg (2000) identified five: <em>attribution of responsibility</em>, <em>conflict</em>, <em>human interest</em>, <em>economic consequences</em>, and <em>morality</em>.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Issue-specific frames</strong></td><td>Frames unique to a particular topic (e.g., a "national security" frame for immigration policy).</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Inductive approach</strong></td><td>Identify frames by reading your sources openly and allowing frames to emerge from the data.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Deductive approach</strong></td><td>Start with predefined frames (from existing literature) and code your sources for their presence.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Episodic vs. thematic framing</strong></td><td>Episodic framing focuses on specific events or individuals; thematic framing places issues in a broader context (Iyengar 1991).</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>When to use it</h4>

<ul>
<li>Your research question asks how an issue, event, or actor is represented in media, policy documents, or political discourse</li>
<li>You are analyzing a corpus of texts (news articles, speeches, social media posts, policy documents)</li>
<li>You want to systematically identify patterns in how issues are presented</li>
<li>You are interested in the relationship between media representation and public understanding or policy outcomes</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>BA vs MA:</strong> BA theses should demonstrate a clear understanding of what frames are and apply a systematic coding approach. MA theses are expected to engage with methodological debates (inductive vs. deductive, reliability, frame element identification) and justify their approach with reference to the literature.</p>

<h4>How to apply it in your thesis</h4>

<ol>
<li><strong>Define your corpus</strong> — What texts are you analyzing? How were they selected? (See <a href="#corpus">Building a Corpus</a> below.)</li>
<li><strong>Choose your approach</strong> — Inductive (frames emerge from data), deductive (use predefined frames), or a combination. Justify your choice.</li>
<li><strong>Develop a codebook</strong> — Define each frame clearly, with inclusion/exclusion criteria and examples. If using Entman's four dimensions, specify what counts as a "problem definition," "causal diagnosis," etc.</li>
<li><strong>Code your texts</strong> — Apply your codebook systematically. For each text, identify which frame(s) are present and record evidence.</li>
<li><strong>Assess reliability</strong> — If possible, have a second coder code a subset of your data. Report inter-coder reliability (Matthes &amp; Kohring 2008).</li>
<li><strong>Analyze patterns</strong> — What frames dominate? How do frames vary across sources, time periods, or outlets? What is absent?</li>
</ol>

<h4>Full article vs. headline framing</h4>

<p>Students often ask whether to code only headlines or entire articles. The answer depends on your research question:</p>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Approach</th><th>When to use it</th><th>Trade-offs</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Full article</strong></td><td>When you need a complete picture of how an issue is framed, including nuance and multiple frames within a single text</td><td>More time-intensive; richer data</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Headlines only</strong></td><td>When studying how issues are initially presented to audiences, or when working with a very large corpus</td><td>Faster; may miss counter-framing within the article</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Headlines + lead paragraphs</strong></td><td>A practical middle ground; captures the dominant frame without requiring full-text coding</td><td>Good balance of depth and feasibility</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<div class="tip-box">
<p><strong>A frame is not a topic.</strong> "Immigration" is a topic. "Immigration as a security threat" is a frame. Frames involve a <em>perspective</em> on the topic — a problem definition, a causal story, a moral evaluation, or a proposed solution. If your "frames" are just topics, you are doing content analysis, not framing analysis.</p>
</div>

<div class="reflection-box">
<p>Ask yourself</p>
<ul>
<li>Are you identifying frames (perspectives on an issue) or topics (what the issue is about)?</li>
<li>Are you using an inductive or deductive approach, and why?</li>
<li>How will you ensure consistency in your coding? Do you have clear frame definitions?</li>
<li>Are you coding full articles, headlines, or something in between? Why?</li>
<li>How will you handle texts that contain multiple frames?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="exercise-box">
<p>Exercise</p>
<p><strong>Part A:</strong> Select 5–10 texts from your corpus. Read them and inductively identify the dominant frames. Write a brief description of each frame (2–3 sentences).</p>
<p><strong>Part B:</strong> Draft a codebook with clear definitions, inclusion criteria, and examples for each frame. Test it on a small sample and note any ambiguities.</p>
<p>Bring both to your next supervision meeting.</p>
</div>

<h4>Key readings</h4>

<ul>
<li><strong>Entman, R. M. (1993).</strong> "Framing: Toward clarification of a fractured paradigm." <em>Journal of Communication</em>, 43(4), 51–58. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1460-2466.1993.tb01304.x">DOI</a> — The foundational definition of framing.</li>
<li><strong>Semetko, H. A., &amp; Valkenburg, P. M. (2000).</strong> "Framing European politics." <em>Journal of Communication</em>, 50(2), 93–109. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1460-2466.2000.tb02843.x">DOI</a> — Introduces the five generic news frames.</li>
<li><strong>Chong, D., &amp; Druckman, J. N. (2007).</strong> "Framing theory." <em>Annual Review of Political Science</em>, 10(1), 103–126. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.polisci.10.072805.103054">DOI</a> — Comprehensive theoretical overview.</li>

</ul>

<details id="discourse-analysis" markdown="0">
<summary>Discourse Analysis</summary>
<div class="details-content">

<p class="step-intro">Discourse analysis examines how language and texts construct meaning, shape identities, and reproduce or challenge power relations. It is widely used in international relations, area studies, and political science — particularly for analyzing how issues are talked about, written about, and contested.</p>

<div class="step-objectives">
<p>Objectives</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand what discourse analysis is and what distinguishes it from content or framing analysis</li>
<li>Know the main approaches: Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA), poststructuralist discourse theory, and IR-specific discourse analysis</li>
<li>Learn how to choose an approach and apply it in your thesis</li>
<li>Understand the relationship between theory and method in discourse analysis</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>What is discourse analysis?</h4>

<p>Discourse analysis studies how language — in texts, speech, images, or other communicative practices — constructs social reality. Unlike content analysis (which counts and categorizes) or framing analysis (which identifies how issues are presented), discourse analysis asks how language <em>constitutes</em> the things it describes: how it produces knowledge, constructs identities, and establishes power relations.</p>

<p>There is no single "discourse analysis method." Instead, there are several distinct approaches, each grounded in different theoretical traditions. Choosing the right one depends on your research question, your data, and your theoretical commitments.</p>

<h4>The main approaches</h4>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Approach</th><th>Key authors</th><th>Core focus</th><th>Best for</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA)</strong></td><td>Fairclough, Wodak, van Dijk</td><td>How language reproduces power, inequality, and ideology</td><td>Analyzing media, policy, political speech; institutional language</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Discourse-Historical Approach</strong></td><td>Wodak</td><td>How discourses develop over time; identity and discrimination</td><td>Historical analysis; nationalism, prejudice, identity construction</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Poststructuralist discourse theory</strong></td><td>Laclau &amp; Mouffe</td><td>How political identities and coalitions are constructed through discourse</td><td>Political identity, hegemony, populism</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Discourse analysis in IR</strong></td><td>Milliken, Hansen, Waever</td><td>How foreign policy, security, and international identity are discursively constructed</td><td>Security studies, foreign policy analysis, IR constructivism</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Argumentative Discourse Analysis</strong></td><td>Hajer</td><td>How actors position themselves through "storylines" and "discourse coalitions"</td><td>Policy analysis, environmental politics, governance</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>Choosing an approach</h4>

<p>The best starting point for students encountering discourse analysis for the first time is Jørgensen and Phillips (2002), which compares three major approaches side by side and helps you identify which fits your research question.</p>

<p>Some practical guidance:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>If your question is about power, ideology, or institutional language</strong> → CDA (Fairclough 1992; Wodak &amp; Meyer 2016)</li>
<li><strong>If your question is about security, foreign policy, or international identity</strong> → IR discourse analysis (Milliken 1999; Hansen 2006)</li>
<li><strong>If your question is about political identity formation or hegemony</strong> → Poststructuralist discourse theory (Laclau &amp; Mouffe 1985; Howarth 2000)</li>
<li><strong>If your question is about how actors argue about policy</strong> → Argumentative discourse analysis (Hajer 1995)</li>
</ul>

<h4>How to apply it in your thesis</h4>

<ol>
<li><strong>Choose and justify your approach</strong> — Which variant of discourse analysis fits your research question? Why? Ground your choice in the literature.</li>
<li><strong>Define your data</strong> — What texts, speeches, documents, or other materials will you analyze? How were they selected?</li>
<li><strong>Specify your analytical strategy</strong> — What exactly are you looking for in the texts? Depending on your approach:
<ul>
<li>CDA: linguistic features (vocabulary, grammar, metaphors) and their social functions</li>
<li>IR discourse analysis: identity constructions, linking/differentiation, policy predictions (Hansen 2006)</li>
<li>Poststructuralist: nodal points, chains of equivalence, moments of closure</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>Conduct the analysis</strong> — Read closely, code systematically, and document your interpretive decisions.</li>
<li><strong>Connect text to context</strong> — Discourse analysis always moves between the micro level (what the text says) and the macro level (the social, political, and historical context).</li>
<li><strong>Present your findings</strong> — Use direct quotations as evidence, explain your interpretations, and connect them to your broader argument.</li>
</ol>

<div class="tip-box">
<p><strong>Discourse analysis is not just "close reading."</strong> It requires an explicit theoretical framework (CDA, poststructuralism, etc.) and a systematic analytical strategy. Simply reading texts and noting interesting patterns is not discourse analysis — you need to specify <em>what</em> you are looking for, <em>why</em>, and <em>how</em> your approach connects to a broader theoretical tradition.</p>
</div>

<div class="reflection-box">
<p>Ask yourself</p>
<ul>
<li>Which approach to discourse analysis fits your research question and data?</li>
<li>What is the relationship between language and social reality in your theoretical framework?</li>
<li>What specific features of the texts will you analyze (vocabulary, metaphors, narrative structures, identity constructions)?</li>
<li>How will you ensure your analysis is systematic rather than impressionistic?</li>
<li>How will you handle the tension between interpretation and evidence?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="exercise-box">
<p>Exercise</p>
<p><strong>Part A:</strong> Read Jørgensen and Phillips (2002), Chapters 1–3. Write a one-paragraph summary of which approach best fits your research question and why.</p>
<p><strong>Part B:</strong> Select 2–3 texts from your data. Apply your chosen approach to a preliminary analysis of one text. Identify the key discursive features (e.g., framing devices, identity constructions, storylines) and explain what they reveal.</p>
<p>Bring both to your next supervision meeting.</p>
</div>

<ul>

## Practical Guides {#practical-guides}

<details id="corpus" markdown="0">
<summary>Building a Corpus</summary>
<div class="details-content">

<p class="step-intro">Many methods — especially framing analysis, discourse analysis, and content analysis — require assembling a collection of texts (a corpus) before analysis can begin. This guide covers how to plan, collect, organize, and manage a corpus for your thesis.</p>

<div class="step-objectives">
<p>Objectives</p>
<ul>
<li>Understand what a corpus is and when you need one</li>
<li>Learn strategies for collecting and organizing textual data</li>
<li>Know the ethical and legal considerations for data collection</li>
<li>Understand how computational tools can assist with corpus management</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>What is a corpus?</h4>

<p>A corpus is a structured, systematically collected body of texts assembled for analysis. It might consist of news articles, government documents, speeches, social media posts, interview transcripts, or any other textual material relevant to your research question.</p>

<p>A corpus is not just a pile of documents. It should be:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Defined</strong> — Clear criteria for what is included and excluded</li>
<li><strong>Systematic</strong> — Collected according to a consistent procedure</li>
<li><strong>Documented</strong> — Selection criteria, sources, and collection methods are recorded</li>
<li><strong>Appropriate</strong> — Representative of the phenomenon you are studying</li>
</ul>

<h4>Planning your corpus</h4>

<p>Before collecting anything, answer these questions:</p>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Question</th><th>Why it matters</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>What is your unit of analysis?</strong></td><td>Individual articles? Paragraphs? Speeches? Social media posts? This determines how you will collect and code.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>What is the time period?</strong></td><td>Are you analyzing coverage during a specific event, over a longer period, or comparing time periods?</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>What are the sources?</strong></td><td>Which outlets, databases, or archives will you draw from? Are they representative?</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>What are your search terms?</strong></td><td>How will you find relevant texts? What keywords, filters, or selection criteria will you use?</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>How large should the corpus be?</strong></td><td>Large enough to identify patterns, small enough to analyze carefully. This depends on your method — framing analysis of full articles might use 50–200 texts; headline analysis might use more.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>Collection methods</h4>

<table>
<thead><tr><th>Method</th><th>When to use it</th><th>Key considerations</th></tr></thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td><strong>Database search</strong></td><td>News articles (LexisNexis, Factiva, ProQuest), academic papers, government documents</td><td>Most reliable for academic work; check your library's access. Document your search terms and filters.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Manual collection</strong></td><td>Small corpora; sources not available in databases; archival materials</td><td>Time-intensive but gives you full control over selection. Keep careful records.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>Web scraping</strong></td><td>Large-scale collection from websites where no database or API exists</td><td>Check legality and terms of service; consider ethical implications (Brown et al. 2025). Respect robots.txt. Always disclose your methods.</td></tr>
<tr><td><strong>APIs</strong></td><td>Social media data, news feeds, structured datasets</td><td>Standardized access with clear terms of use (Lomborg &amp; Bechmann 2014). API availability changes frequently — check current status.</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h4>Organizing your corpus</h4>

<p>Good organization saves enormous amounts of time later. Follow these principles:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Consistent file naming:</strong> Use a predictable format. Example: <code>YYYY-MM-DD_Source_Title.pdf</code></li>
<li><strong>Metadata tracking:</strong> Maintain a spreadsheet or database with at minimum: title, source, date, author, URL/DOI, and any relevant tags.</li>
<li><strong>FAIR principles:</strong> Make your data Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable. See <a href="{{ '/getting-started/#step-2' | relative_url }}">Getting Started — Step 2</a> for details.</li>
<li><strong>Backup:</strong> Keep copies in at least two locations.</li>
</ul>

<h4>Using computational tools</h4>

<p>For larger corpora, computational tools can help with collection, cleaning, and organization. Common tools include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Reference managers</strong> (Zotero, Mendeley) — for tracking bibliographic metadata and PDFs</li>
<li><strong>Spreadsheets</strong> — for metadata tracking and basic corpus management</li>
<li><strong>Python libraries</strong> — for automated collection and text processing (e.g., text extraction from PDFs, cleaning, standardizing formats)</li>
<li><strong>AI-assisted tools</strong> — for batch processing tasks like PDF conversion, text cleaning, or organizing large document collections</li>
</ul>

<div class="info-box">
<p><strong>Important:</strong> Using computational tools for data <em>management</em> (collecting, cleaning, organizing) is different from using them to <em>write</em> your thesis. Any use of AI tools must comply with the <a href="{{ '/ethics/#generative-ai-policy' | relative_url }}">Generative AI Policy</a>. Discuss tool use with your supervisor and always disclose your methods transparently.</p>
</div>

<div class="tip-box">
<p><strong>Document everything.</strong> Your corpus construction is part of your methodology. A reader should be able to understand exactly how and why you selected the texts you did. If you used automated tools for collection or processing, describe them. If you made judgment calls about inclusion or exclusion, explain them.</p>
</div>

<div class="reflection-box">
<p>Ask yourself</p>
<ul>
<li>What are your inclusion and exclusion criteria? Can you state them precisely?</li>
<li>Is your corpus representative of the phenomenon you are studying, or is it biased toward certain sources, time periods, or perspectives?</li>
<li>How will you handle texts in multiple languages?</li>
<li>Do you have the rights and access to use these texts for research purposes?</li>
<li>How will you organize and store your corpus so that your analysis is reproducible?</li>
</ul>
</div>

<div class="exercise-box">
<p>Exercise</p>
<p>Write a corpus construction plan (300–500 words) that includes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Your unit of analysis and source(s)</li>
<li>Your time period and search terms or selection criteria</li>
<li>Your collection method (database, manual, scraping, API)</li>
<li>Your target corpus size and justification</li>
<li>Your plan for organizing and documenting the corpus</li>
</ol>
<p>Bring this to your next supervision meeting.</p>
</div>

<h4>Key readings</h4>

<ul>
<li><strong>Krippendorff, K. (2018).</strong> <em>Content Analysis: An Introduction to Its Methodology</em> (4th ed.). Sage. — The standard methodological reference for systematic text analysis.</li>
<li><strong>Stefanowitsch, A. (2020).</strong> <em>Corpus Linguistics: A Guide to the Methodology.</em> Language Science Press. — Comprehensive open-access guide. <a href="https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.3735822">Free PDF</a>.</li>
<li><strong>Brown, M. A., et al. (2025).</strong> "Web scraping for research: Legal, ethical, institutional, and scientific considerations." <em>Big Data &amp; Society</em>, 12(4). <a href="https://doi.org/10.1177/20539517251381686">DOI</a> — Essential reading if you are scraping data from the web.</li>
<li><strong>Lomborg, S., &amp; Bechmann, A. (2014).</strong> "Using APIs for data collection on social media." <em>The Information Society</em>, 30(4), 256–265. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1080/01972243.2014.915276">DOI</a> — Key article on API-based data collection.</li>
</ul>

## Other Methods to Explore {#other-methods}

The methods above are covered in depth because they are especially common in the programs I supervise. But they are far from the only options. Depending on your research question, you may also want to consider:

| Method | Brief description | Common in |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Qualitative interviewing + thematic analysis** | Semi-structured interviews analyzed for recurring themes and patterns | All programs, especially fieldwork-based theses |
| **Content analysis** | Systematic coding and categorization of textual material; can be quantitative or qualitative | IR, media-adjacent topics |
| **Survey methods / quantitative analysis** | Statistical analysis of original or secondary survey data | MAIR especially, BAIS with quantitative focus |
| **Archival research** | Systematic analysis of historical documents, government records, correspondence | Korean Studies, history-focused MAAS theses |

Discuss your methodological choices with your supervisor early. The [Getting Started Guide — Step 4]({{ '/getting-started/#step-4' | relative_url }}) covers method selection in general terms.
