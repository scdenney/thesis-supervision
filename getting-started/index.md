# Getting Started

This guide walks you through the key stages of planning and writing a thesis — from formulating your research question to presenting your findings. It applies to all programs (BAIS, BAKS, MAAS, MAIR) and is designed to be worked through sequentially, though you can revisit any step at any time.

---

## How to Use This Guide

Click on each step below to expand it. Each step includes:

- **Objectives** — What you should aim to accomplish
- **Key concepts** — Core ideas to understand
- **Guiding questions** — Prompts to help you think through the step
- **Exercise** — A concrete task to move your thesis forward

Work through the steps in order. By the end, you should have a working research question, a data collection plan, a literature review outline, a methodological framework, and a strategy for writing up your findings.

---

## The Five Steps {#the-five-steps}

<details id="step-1">
<summary>Step 1: Developing Your Research Question</summary>
<div class="details-content" markdown="1">

#### Objectives

- Understand what makes a good research question
- Learn how to identify a research problem or gap in the literature
- Begin thinking about the structure of your thesis

#### Key Concepts

**The research question** is the foundation of your thesis. A strong research question is:

- **Clear** — It can be understood without additional explanation
- **Focused** — It is narrow enough to be answered within the scope of your thesis
- **Relevant** — It connects to existing scholarly debates in your field
- **Researchable** — It can be investigated with available data and methods

**The research problem** is the broader issue or gap that your question addresses. It explains *why* your question matters — what we don't yet know, what existing scholarship has overlooked, or what needs to be re-examined.

**The research gap** is the specific hole in the literature that your thesis aims to fill. Gaps can take many forms: an understudied case, an untested theory, a missing comparison, a neglected perspective, or new data that warrants fresh analysis.

#### How Research Questions Evolve

Your research question will almost certainly change as you read more and refine your thinking. This is normal and expected. Start with a working question that gives you direction, and plan to revise it as your understanding deepens.

#### Guiding Questions

- What topic or issue genuinely interests you?
- What do scholars already know about this topic? What don't they know?
- Can your question be answered with the data and methods available to you?
- Is the scope realistic for a thesis of your program's word count?
- Does your question contribute to a broader scholarly conversation?

#### Exercise

Draft a working research question (1–2 sentences). Then write a short paragraph (150–250 words) explaining:
1. The research problem your question addresses
2. Why this question matters (the gap in existing knowledge)
3. How you might begin to answer it

Bring this to your first supervision meeting for discussion.

<details id="step-2">
<summary>Step 2: Data and Sources</summary>
<div class="details-content" markdown="1">

#### Objectives

- Understand the difference between primary and secondary sources
- Learn about the FAIR principles for research data management
- Develop a preliminary plan for data collection

#### Key Concepts

**Primary sources** are original materials that you analyze directly — interviews, surveys, archival documents, government reports, media sources, datasets, literary texts, visual materials, etc.

**Secondary sources** are scholarly works that analyze, interpret, or discuss primary sources — journal articles, monographs, book chapters, and review essays.

Most theses use a combination of both. The balance depends on your research question and methodology.

**The FAIR Principles** provide a framework for responsible data management:

| Principle | Meaning | In practice |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Findable** | Data should be easy to locate | Organize and label your sources systematically; use reference management software |
| **Accessible** | Data should be obtainable | Ensure you can legally and practically access the sources you need |
| **Interoperable** | Data should work across systems | Use standard formats and consistent naming conventions |
| **Reusable** | Data should be usable by others | Document your sources, methods, and any transformations clearly |

#### Types of Data to Consider

- **Textual sources**: policy documents, news articles, speeches, legal texts, literary works
- **Quantitative data**: statistical datasets, survey results, economic indicators
- **Qualitative data**: interview transcripts, ethnographic observations, focus groups
- **Archival materials**: historical documents, correspondence, organizational records
- **Digital sources**: social media data, web content, digital archives

#### Guiding Questions

- What type of data do you need to answer your research question?
- Is this data available and accessible? Are there language requirements?
- How will you collect or access this data?
- Are there ethical considerations (e.g., human participants, sensitive data)? See the [Ethics & Policies]({{ '/ethics/' | relative_url }}) page for guidance.
- How will you organize and manage your data throughout the project?

#### Exercise

Write a data collection plan (300–500 words) that addresses:
1. What types of sources (primary and secondary) you will use
2. How you will find and access these sources
3. How your data plan aligns with the FAIR principles
4. Any potential challenges or limitations in data access

<details id="step-3">
<summary>Step 3: Conducting a Literature Review</summary>
<div class="details-content" markdown="1">

#### Objectives

- Understand the purpose and structure of a literature review
- Learn how to connect the literature review to your research question and gap
- Begin building your literature review

#### Key Concepts

The literature review is **not** a summary of everything written on your topic. It is a critical, organized discussion of the scholarship most relevant to your research question. A good literature review:

- **Summarizes** existing work on your topic
- **Identifies gaps** — what has been overlooked, understudied, or contested
- **Frames your research** — shows how your thesis connects to and builds on existing scholarship
- **Justifies your approach** — explains why your question, data, and methods are appropriate

#### Organizational Strategies

There are several ways to structure a literature review:

- **Thematic**: Organized by topic or theme (most common)
- **Chronological**: Traces the development of scholarship over time
- **Methodological**: Groups studies by the methods they use
- **Theoretical**: Organized around competing theoretical perspectives

Choose the approach that best serves your argument. Many literature reviews combine elements of more than one strategy.

#### Building Your Review Step by Step

1. **Identify key search terms** related to your topic, question, and field
2. **Search systematically** using academic databases (Google Scholar, JSTOR, Web of Science, your field's specialized databases)
3. **Read strategically** — start with abstracts and introductions to assess relevance before reading fully
4. **Take structured notes** — for each source, record the main argument, methods, findings, and how it relates to your research
5. **Group and synthesize** — organize sources into themes, debates, or approaches
6. **Identify the gap** — articulate what the existing literature does not address

#### Guiding Questions

- What are the main scholarly debates surrounding your topic?
- Where do scholars agree? Where do they disagree?
- What methodological approaches have been used? What approaches are missing?
- How does your research question address something the existing literature has not?
- Are there geographic, temporal, or thematic blind spots in the literature?

#### Exercise

Create an annotated bibliography of 8–12 key sources. For each source, write 3–4 sentences covering:
1. The main argument or finding
2. The methods used
3. How the source relates to your research question

Then draft an outline of your literature review, organized by theme, chronology, or methodology.

<details id="step-4">
<summary>Step 4: Building Your Analytical Framework</summary>
<div class="details-content" markdown="1">

#### Objectives

- Understand what an analytical framework is and why it matters
- Learn how to select and justify appropriate methods
- Connect your framework to your research question and data

#### Key Concepts

Your **analytical framework** is the bridge between your research question and your findings. It specifies:

- **What** you are analyzing (your data or cases)
- **How** you will analyze it (your methods and approach)
- **Why** this approach is appropriate for your question

The framework should flow logically from your literature review: the gap you identified should motivate your research design, and your methods should be suited to the type of data you are working with.

#### Common Approaches

**Qualitative methods:**
- Thematic analysis — identifying patterns and themes in textual data
- Content analysis — systematic coding and categorization of textual material
- Discourse analysis — examining how language constructs meaning
- Case study analysis — in-depth examination of one or more cases
- Process tracing — tracking causal mechanisms through detailed evidence

**Quantitative methods:**
- Descriptive statistics — summarizing patterns in numerical data
- Inferential statistics — testing hypotheses about relationships between variables
- Comparative analysis — systematic comparison across cases or datasets

**Mixed methods** combine qualitative and quantitative approaches. This can strengthen your analysis but also increases complexity — discuss with your supervisor whether a mixed-methods design is appropriate for your project.

#### Justifying Your Choices

Your thesis should explicitly explain:

1. Why you chose this particular methodology
2. How it suits your research question and data
3. What its limitations are (and how you account for them)
4. How your analysis will be structured (e.g., by theme, by case, by variable)

#### Guiding Questions

- What type of analysis does your research question require?
- What methods are commonly used in your field for this type of question?
- Do you have the skills and resources to apply your chosen method?
- What are the limitations of your approach, and how will you address them?
- How will you structure your findings chapter?

#### Exercise

Write a methods outline (300–500 words) that includes:
1. Your research design (qualitative, quantitative, or mixed)
2. Your specific method(s) and why they are appropriate
3. How you will structure the analysis
4. Key limitations and how you plan to address them

<details id="step-5">
<summary>Step 5: Writing and Reporting Findings</summary>
<div class="details-content" markdown="1">

#### Objectives

- Understand how to structure and present your findings
- Learn how to connect results back to your research question and literature
- Develop a strategy for writing the full thesis

#### Key Concepts

The findings chapter is where you present the results of your analysis. It should be **organized**, **evidence-based**, and clearly **connected** to your research question.

#### Structuring Your Findings

Your findings can be organized in several ways:

- **By theme** — group results around the key themes that emerged from your analysis
- **By case** — present findings for each case study separately, then compare
- **By research sub-question** — address each sub-question in turn
- **Chronologically** — trace developments over time

Whatever structure you choose, each section should:
1. State what you found
2. Present the evidence (quotes, data, examples)
3. Analyze what the evidence means
4. Connect it to your research question and existing literature

#### From Findings to Conclusions

Your conclusion chapter should:

- **Summarize** your main findings in relation to the research question
- **Discuss contributions** — what does your research add to the field?
- **Acknowledge limitations** honestly — what couldn't you do, and why?
- **Suggest future research** — what questions remain open?

Avoid introducing new evidence or arguments in the conclusion.

#### Writing Strategies

- **Start with what you know best** — you don't have to write chapters in order. Many students find it easiest to start with the literature review or methods section.
- **Write regularly** — consistent daily or weekly writing sessions are more productive than marathon sessions before deadlines.
- **Revise iteratively** — first drafts are meant to be revised. Write a rough version, then improve it.
- **Use feedback** — bring drafts to supervision meetings. Feedback is most useful when it comes early, not at the last minute.
- **Cite as you write** — don't leave citations for later. Use reference management software (Zotero, Mendeley, etc.) to keep track.

#### Formatting Reminders

- All programs use **Chicago Notes-Bibliography** citation style
- Check your program page for specific word count and formatting requirements
- Consult the [Leiden University Library: Citing](https://www.library.universiteitleiden.nl/students/citing) page for citation resources

#### Guiding Questions

- How will you organize your findings — by theme, case, or sub-question?
- What is the key evidence for each of your main points?
- How do your findings relate to the literature you reviewed?
- What are the most important contributions of your research?
- What limitations should you acknowledge?

#### Exercise

1. Draft an outline of your findings chapter, with section headings and brief notes on the evidence you will present in each section
2. Write a rough draft of your conclusion (500–750 words), addressing: summary of findings, contributions, limitations, and future research

## Next Steps

Once you have worked through all five steps, you should have:

- A refined research question
- A data collection plan
- An annotated bibliography and literature review outline
- A methods outline
- A plan for structuring your findings and conclusion

Bring these materials to your supervision meetings. Your supervisor will help you refine your approach and keep you on track toward your deadline.
